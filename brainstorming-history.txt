● For #7 specifically, here are ideas ranked by winning potential:

  ---
  Idea 1 — "The False Positive Killer" (highest ceiling)

  The insight everyone misses: Every team will build a transit detector. The
  hard, unsolved, scorable problem is distinguishing real planets from impostors
  — eclipsing binaries and blends mimic transit signals almost perfectly.
  ISRO's eval explicitly scores classification accuracy across all 4 classes.

  What you build:
  - BLS/TLS for period finding (standard, but necessary)
  - Feature engineering specifically targeting false positive signatures:
  odd/even transit depth asymmetry (eclipsing binary tell), secondary eclipse
  presence, centroid shift proxy, transit shape (V vs U shape)
  - LightGBM 4-class classifier (transit / eclipse / blend / other) with SHAP
  per-prediction explanations
  - Trapezoid model fitting for parameter estimation with uncertainty bounds
  - Streamlit dashboard: folded light curve + classification breakdown + "why
  this isn't a planet" reasoning

  Why it wins: Judges are ISRO scientists who know the false positive problem is
  what kills real telescope time. A system that explains why it rejected a
  candidate is more valuable than one that just finds transits.

  ---
  Idea 2 — "Ensemble + Uncertainty" (safer, strong)

  Two-model ensemble where both models must agree to flag a candidate:
  - Model A: Classical features → LightGBM
  - Model B: Raw flux sequence → 1D CNN (runs free on Colab)
  - Disagreement = flagged as "uncertain", confidence score output
  - Parameter estimation via scipy curve fitting on phase-folded light curve

  Why it wins: The eval explicitly asks for SNR/significance levels. Most teams
  will output a binary label. You output a calibrated confidence score with
  uncertainty — that's what real science requires.

  ---
  Idea 3 — "Visual Clarity" angle (easiest to execute, lower ceiling)

  The 4th eval criterion is visualization and clarity. If you can't beat others
  on accuracy, beat them on the demo:
  - Interactive folded light curve with transit model overlaid
  - Side-by-side comparison: raw flux vs detrended vs phase-folded
  - Parameter estimation displayed with error bars
  - One-click report generation (the 3-page report they require)

  This alone won't win but combined with Idea 1 or 2 it's the polish that pushes
  you from 2nd to 1st.

  ---
  My honest take

  Idea 1 is the winning idea. Here's why: the problem statement literally lists
  "blends" and "eclipsing binaries" as separate classes — that's ISRO telling
  you they care about false positive rejection. No team that just runs BLS + a
  basic classifier will handle all 4 classes well. If you build the false
  positive feature engineering correctly, your classification accuracy will be
  meaningfully higher than competitors, and SHAP explanations give judges
  something to engage with in the demo.

  The entire stack is: lightkurve + astropy + scipy + LightGBM + SHAP +
  Streamlit. All free, all runs locally or on Colab.

  ---
  What every basic team will do

  - Download TESS data → run BLS for period finding → binary classifier (planet
  / not-planet) → output period/depth/duration from BLS → matplotlib plots

  This loses on criterion 1 immediately because the problem explicitly requires
  4 classes: transits / eclipses / blends / other. Teams that treat it as binary
  are already wrong.

  ---
  Where the real differentiation lives

  1. The 4-class problem is the actual challenge

  Each false positive class has specific discriminating signatures that most
  teams won't engineer features for:

  Eclipsing binaries:
  - Odd/even transit depth asymmetry — in binary systems, alternating transits
  have different depths
  - Secondary eclipse at half the period
  - V-shaped transit profile (vs U-shaped for real planets)

  Blends:
  - Centroid shift during transit — the photocenter moves if contamination from
  a nearby star
  - Depth inconsistency across aperture sizes

  Starspots:
  - Non-periodic, asymmetric ingress/egress
  - Longer duration relative to depth

  If you engineer these as explicit features, your 4-class accuracy is
  structurally higher than a team that just throws raw flux into a neural net.

  ---
  2. TLS over BLS

  Most teams default to BLS (Box Least Squares). TLS (Transit Least Squares)
  uses a physically realistic transit shape model instead of a box — it has
  measurably better sensitivity to real transits and is a single library swap
  (transitleastsquares). This alone improves detection accuracy.

  ---
  3. Parameter estimation is scored separately

  BLS gives rough parameter estimates. Proper trapezoid or batman model fitting
  gives accurate depth/duration with uncertainty bounds. Most teams will submit
  BLS point estimates. You submit fitted parameters with error bars — that's the
  second eval criterion.

  ---
  4. The confidence/SNR criterion is underinvested by everyone

  The problem asks for "significance levels of identified events." Most teams
  will output a classifier probability and call it done. What actually answers
  this:
  - BLS power spectrum peak significance (bootstrap or analytical false alarm
  probability)
  - Calibrated classifier probability (Platt scaling — sklearn one-liner)
  - Parameter fit uncertainties

  ---
  5. Scale is an implicit test

  20-30k light curves. Judges will notice if your demo only shows 10
  cherry-picked stars. A pipeline that actually runs on the full sector is a
  different class of submission.

  ---
  Three distinct approaches

  Approach A — "False Positive First"
  Everything optimized for 4-class classification accuracy. Deep false-positive
  feature engineering (odd/even test, secondary eclipse, centroid proxy, V-shape
  score). LightGBM + SHAP explanations. Win on criterion 1.
  Risk: parameter estimation is secondary.

  Approach B — "Full Pipeline"
  Build something that actually works end-to-end on all 20-30k light curves.
  Every component handles messy real data. Strong visualization. Win on criteria
  3 and 4.
  Risk: individual component accuracy may not be state of the art.

  Approach C — "Uncertainty First"
  Bayesian everything. MCMC parameter estimation. Calibrated probabilities.

  Risk: MCMC on 30k light curves is computationally expensive. Colab will

  ---
  What the pipeline actually needs to handle

  Per flare event:
  - Pre-flare window: 30–60 min before peak (precursor extraction)
  - Flare window: onset → peak → decay
  - Post-flare quiet: baseline re-establishment

  - SoLEXS (soft X-ray, low energy) — time-series at second-level cadence
  - HEL1OS (hard X-ray, high energy) — time-series

  - Cons: feature engineering is manual, may miss complex patterns

  - Pros: potentially better accuracy, modern approach
  - Cons: needs more training data, less interpretable, Colab free tier will
  struggle with training time

  - Nowcasting = classical derivative threshold (fast, reliable, high TPR)
  - Forecasting = LightGBM on engineered features including the hard/soft ratio
  - Best of both: nowcasting doesn't fail on edge cases, forecasting has the ML
