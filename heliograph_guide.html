B Why this matters Science

A solar flare is a sudden release of magnetic energy on the Sun that floods space with radiation across the electromagnetic spectrum. When that radiation and the particles/plasma that follow it reach Earth, they cause space weather — and space weather breaks the infrastructure modern life runs on.

📡

Satellite communications

X-ray and EUV bursts ionise the upper atmosphere within ~8 minutes, causing radio blackouts and degrading or knocking out satellite links — including the very satellites ISRO operates.
🛰️

GPS / NavIC navigation

Sudden ionospheric changes corrupt the timing signals positioning depends on, throwing off navigation for aviation, shipping, defence and everyday devices.
⚡

Power grids

The geomagnetic storms that can follow large flares induce currents in long transmission lines, tripping protection and, in extremes, damaging transformers.
👩‍🚀

Astronaut & spacecraft safety

Hard-X-ray and energetic-particle events are a radiation hazard to crewed missions (think Gaganyaan) and can flip bits or damage electronics on spacecraft.
The ask, in one line
If we can detect a flare the moment it starts (nowcast) and — better — warn minutes before its most damaging peak (forecast), operators get time to safe-mode hardware. That warning time is literally the thing ISRO scores. Everything in this guide optimises for it.

C The Sun & solar flares, from scratch Science

You don't need a physics degree — just four ideas.

1 · The Sun is a magnetic machine

The Sun is a ball of plasma (charged gas). Moving charges make magnetic fields, and the Sun's rotation twists those fields into stressed, tangled loops above active regions (sunspots). Stressed magnetic fields store energy like a wound spring.

2 · Magnetic reconnection releases the energy

When tangled field lines snap and reconnect into a lower-energy configuration, the stored energy is released explosively — accelerating electrons to enormous speeds and heating local plasma to tens of millions of kelvin. That event is a flare.

3 · A flare has two phases

⚡ Impulsive phase (seconds–minutes)

Newly-accelerated non-thermal electrons slam into the dense lower atmosphere and emit hard X-rays (high energy). Short, spiky, early.

🔥 Gradual phase (minutes–hours)

The plasma those electrons heated glows in soft X-rays (lower energy) — a smooth rise to a peak, then a slow decay. This is the bulk thermal radiation.

4 · Flares come in sizes — the A/B/C/M/X scale

Flares are classified by their peak soft-X-ray brightness (the GOES 1–8 Å flux). It's a logarithmic ladder — each letter is 10× the previous.

D X-rays: soft vs hard Science

X-rays are light — just very high-energy light, far beyond visible. We measure their energy in kilo-electron-volts (keV). Higher keV = "harder" = more penetrating.

Soft X-rays = "how much is burning"

Thermal radiation from heated plasma. Tracks the accumulated energy — the flare's total thermal output. Rises and peaks after the action. SoLEXS measures this.

Hard X-rays = "how fast it's igniting"

Non-thermal radiation from freshly-accelerated electrons. Tracks the instantaneous energy input — it spikes during the explosive moment, early. HEL1OS measures this.
Why this distinction is the whole game
Soft tells you a flare is happening. Hard tells you it's happening right now, fast — before the soft-X-ray brightness has peaked. The relationship between them (the Neupert effect, §E2) is exactly what buys us forecast lead time. This is why fusing both payloads beats either one alone.

E Aditya-L1 & its X-ray payloads Science

Where is it, and why there?

Aditya-L1 is ISRO's first dedicated solar mission. It doesn't orbit Earth — it sits at the Sun–Earth Lagrange point L1, about 1.5 million km sunward of Earth, where the Sun's and Earth's gravity balance so the spacecraft keeps pace with Earth. From L1 it gets an uninterrupted, never-eclipsed view of the Sun — perfect for continuous monitoring.

SoLEXS — Solar Low Energy X-ray Spectrometer

  • Band: 2–22 keV (soft)
  • Cadence: 1 second
  • Two detectors: SDD1 (small 0.1 mm² — handles bright/large flares without saturating) & SDD2 (large 7.1 mm² — sensitive to the faint quiet Sun)
  • Why two: together they span the A→X dynamic range

HEL1OS — High Energy L1 Orbiting X-ray Spectrometer

  • Band: 8–150 keV (hard)
  • Detectors: CdTe (8–70 keV) + CZT (20–150 keV)
  • FoV: 6°×6° collimator
  • Role here: the early-warning channel — fires during the impulsive phase

E2 Anatomy of a light curve + the Neupert effect Science → Engineering

A light curve is just brightness (here: X-ray counts per second) plotted against time. Below is the real SoLEXS light curve from the day-file we already downloaded (2026-06-15, detector SDD2). Quiet Sun sits near 7 counts/s; a real enhancement reaches ~95 counts/s around 13:15 UTC.

The Neupert effect — the physical basis for forecasting

Observed empirically and explained by flare physics: the hard-X-ray light curve looks like the time-derivative (rate of rise) of the soft-X-ray light curve. Equivalently, soft X-rays are the running integral of the hard. ~80% of large flares obey it.

HXR(t)  ≈  d/dt SXR(t)   ⟺   SXR(t)  ≈  ∫ HXR dt

Be honest about what "forecasting" means here
Two horizons, two confidence levels:
• Short-horizon peak forecasting (defensible & strong): Neupert gives real minutes of warning before the damaging soft-X-ray peak, because the hard channel already told us energy release began.
• Longer-horizon onset forecasting (research frontier): predicting a flare before any rise relies on subtle pre-flare microflares/brightenings. We pursue it, but report it with calibrated uncertainty — we never claim more lead time than the physics supports.

F The data — what we have & how to read it Engineering

FITS, in 30 seconds

FITS (Flexible Image Transport System) is astronomy's standard file format: human-readable 80-character header "cards" in 2880-byte blocks, followed by binary data. A light curve lives in a binary table extension with named columns. You read it with astropy.io.fits — or, as we did to embed the real sample above, by parsing the blocks directly.

The exact structure of our SoLEXS day-file

From AL1_SOLEXS_20260615_SDD2_L1.lc.gz, decoded directly:

PropertyValueMeaning
EXTNAMERATE (OGIP LIGHTCURVE)standard light-curve table
ColumnsTIME (f64), COUNTS (f64)timestamp + counts/s
TIMEDEL11-second cadence
NAXIS286,400exactly one 24-h day
TIME epochUnix sec (MJDREF 40587)1970 epoch → trivial to convert

Day max95 counts/s @ 13:15 UTCa real enhancement (13.6× quiet)

Sibling files per day/detector:

  • .lc — the light curve (what we model)
  • .gti — Good-Time-Intervals: the windows where data is valid. Always apply this to mask gaps/occultations before analysis (our file had only 2 NaN seconds — clean, but never assume).
  • .pi — the energy spectrum (not needed for the light-curve task)

HEL1OS ships separately as HLS_YYYYMMDD_..._lev1.zip (~12 h light curves). The pipeline merges SoLEXS (soft) and HEL1OS (hard) onto a common 1-second grid.

Labels: where "ground truth" comes from

We don't have to label flares by hand. NOAA's GOES X-ray flare catalog (free) lists every flare's start/peak/end time and class (A→X). Because Aditya-L1 at L1 sees the Sun essentially simultaneously with Earth-orbit GOES (a few light-seconds difference — negligible), those timestamps are valid labels for our data. Cross-reference catalog times → Aditya-L1 files → flare / pre-flare / quiet windows.

Rigour note · counts ≠ flux
SoLEXS gives counts/s, not GOES W/m². So we classify relative magnitude from SoLEXS and use GOES for the ground-truth class label. State this assumption openly in the report — don't silently pretend counts are flux.

The 70 GB problem — and the smarter answer

The full archive is ~70 GB. Don't brute-force it. Use the GOES catalog to download only flare windows + their pre-flare windows + sampled quiet windows ≈ 4–6 GB onto the 256 GB external drive. Targeted selection is faster and a stronger methodology story for the report.

⚠️ Environment blocker we already hit — fix it first
This machine has Python 3.14, on which astropy has no prebuilt wheel and fails to compile (needs MSVC). Create a Python 3.11 environment before anything else: 
# with uv (fast) — or use conda
uv venv --python 3.11 .venv
.venv\Scripts\activate
uv pip install astropy numpy pandas scipy lightgbm scikit-learn matplotlib plotly streamlit sktime
Everything in §O assumes this 3.11 env.

L Methodology & rigour Engineering

Every choice below is justified against ISRO's stated evaluation criteria, not convenience. This is the "fight-back" section — where we argue why, not just what.

Two problems, two right tools

Nowcasting → classical, not ML

Detecting an ongoing flare is a solved signal-processing problem: estimate an adaptive quiet-Sun baseline (rolling median), then flag a sharp rise-rate above it with persistence/hysteresis to kill false triggers. Run it independently on soft and hard, then merge into one master catalogue.

Why not a classifier? Operational catalogues (incl. GOES event lists) are built exactly this way. It's interpretable, needs no training, generalises, and — with an adaptive baseline — catches both faint A/B/C and giant M/X flares. A black-box here would be strictly worse: data-hungry and prone to missing the rare strong events that matter most.

Forecasting → hybrid ensemble

Branch 1 · LightGBM on engineered, Neupert-grounded features (hard/soft ratio + its derivative, soft-flux slope & curvature, variance, background level, time-since-last-flare). Interpretable via SHAP — it can tell you which precursor fired the alert.

Branch 2 · MiniROCKET on the raw dual-channel window — random convolutional kernels + a linear head — to learn precursor morphology the hand-features miss.

Fusion · calibrated probability combine → one P(flare in next N min).

Why MiniROCKET and not a deep CNN/Transformer?
ISRO rewards precursor-pattern identification and detection across the dynamic range, so a raw-sequence learner earns its place. But flares — especially M/X — are rare, and a heavy deep net would overfit that minority class and strain a 4 GB-VRAM RTX 3050. MiniROCKET is deterministic, CPU-only, fits in seconds, and is robust on small/imbalanced data — near state-of-the-art on time-series classification without the overfitting or hardware tax. It's the minimal addition that satisfies the criteria. (A small 1D-CNN is kept as an optional GPU experiment, not the primary.)

Evaluation — the part most teams get wrong

Flares are a severe class-imbalance problem (mostly "no flare"). Plain accuracy is meaningless — a model that always says "no flare" scores ~99%. The space-weather standard is the True Skill Statistic:

TSS = TPR − FPR

Reported alongside TSS: HSS, ROC-AUC, Brier score + a reliability diagram (are the probabilities calibrated?), and a lead-time distribution (how many minutes before the peak did we fire?). Cross-validation is strictly time-ordered / walk-forward — never random shuffling, which would leak the future into the past.

G Opportunity & USP ISRO Submission · template p3

How is it different from existing ideas?

Most flare tools use a single channel (usually soft X-rays) from Earth-orbit satellites, and most "forecasts" are really detections. HELIOGRAPH is different on three axes:

🔗

Dual-payload fusion

It combines SoLEXS soft + HEL1OS hard from the L1 vantage — exploiting both channels, not one.
⏱️

Physics-grounded lead time

It forecasts the damaging soft-X-ray peak using the Neupert effect — a real mechanism, not a curve-fit.
📏

Honest, standard evaluation

TSS-driven, calibrated, time-blocked — the metrics the space-weather community actually trusts.

How will it solve the problem?

Nowcasting gives operators an instant, automated flare catalogue. Forecasting gives them the one thing they can't buy otherwise — minutes of warning before the peak — to safe-mode satellites, hold GPS-critical operations and protect grids. It runs offline on commodity hardware, so it's deployable anywhere ISRO needs it.

USP in one sentence
The only dual-payload, L1-vantage flare system that turns the Neupert effect into calibrated, TSS-validated lead-time warnings — fully reproducible at ₹0 on a laptop.
